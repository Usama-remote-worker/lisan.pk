## The Cost of a Clerical Error: Why USCIS Rejects Self-Translated Marriage Documents

A frequent misconception among bilingual applicants is that they can save time by self-translating their marriage contracts or using free automated platforms like Google Translate. However, federal regulation under **8 CFR § 103.2(b)(3)** strictly outlaws this practice. USCIS requires all foreign language documents to be translated by an impartial, professional third party who can legally certify their work. Self-translations, even if structurally flawless, trigger automatic rejections.



To secure a true translation status that passes US border and immigration filters on the first review, the translated text must be accompanied by a dedicated Certificate of Translation Accuracy. According to official USCIS document filing guidelines, this certificate must be a standalone page containing:

- [x] A formal statement declaring the translator’s absolute linguistic competency in both Urdu and English.
- [x] A clear pledge confirming that the translation is a complete and accurate rendering of the original record.
- [x] The translator's or authorized agency representative's printed name, handwritten signature, and date.
- [x] The agency's physical office address and corporate contact details for verification.

Furthermore, leading immigration platforms report that over 60% of translation revisions are caused by a direct lack of name alignment between foreign civil records and modern international passports. ## Essential Checklist of Urdu Marriage Fields Required by USCIS

When reviewing your traditional Urdu marriage contract, verify that your service provider is translating these foundational data blocks, as an omission will compromise your application:

* **Identity & Demographics:** Full legal names, ages, addresses, and parentage details for both the bride and the groom (Columns 1 through 6).
* **Solemnization Metrics:** The exact date of the marriage ceremony along with the specific geographic location (Columns 11 and 12).
* **The Mehr (Dower Ledger):** The exact financial terms, including what portion is prompt (Mu'ajjal) and what portion is deferred (Mu'awajjal) (Columns 13 through 16).
* **Legal Authorization & Conditions:** Special conditions or delegated rights of divorce (Tafweez-e-Talaq), alongside the names, signatures, and stamps of the witnesses, vakils, and the registering Nikah Registrar.
