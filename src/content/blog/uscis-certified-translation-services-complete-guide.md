## What Are USCIS Certified Translation Services?

USCIS certified translation services provide professionally translated documents accompanied by a signed certification statement confirming that:

* The translation is complete
* The translation is accurate
* The translator is competent to translate between both languages

USCIS does not approve specific translation companies. Instead, the submitted translation package must meet the requirements outlined in federal regulations.

Today, translation quality is more important than ever. According to the Migration Policy Institute, nearly 49.8 million people in the United States have limited English proficiency (LEP). This highlights the growing need for accurate language services during immigration processes.

## USCIS Translation Requirements Explained

### Understanding 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3)

The governing regulation states:

> "Any document containing foreign language submitted to USCIS shall be accompanied by a full English language translation which the translator has certified as complete and accurate."

This means USCIS requires:

* A complete English translation
* No summaries or partial translations
* Translator certification of accuracy
* Translator certification of competency

### What Must Be Included?

A compliant USCIS translation package typically includes:

1. Original document copy
2. Complete English translation
3. Translator certification statement
4. Translator signature
5. Translation date
6. Translator contact information

According to USCIS translation guidance, the translation must cover all visible content, including stamps, seals, notes, and annotations whenever applicable.

## Certified vs Notarized Translation: What's the Difference?

One of the biggest misconceptions involves notarization.

### Certified Translation
**Required by USCIS.**

Includes:
* Translation
* Certification statement
* Translator declaration

### Notarized Translation
**Usually NOT required by USCIS.**

Notarization only confirms the identity of the signer. It does not validate translation quality. Many applicants spend extra money on notarization unnecessarily. The primary USCIS requirement remains compliance with 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3).

## Common Translation Mistakes That Can Delay Immigration Cases

Avoid these costly errors:

### 1. Using Machine Translation
Google Translate or AI-generated translations can create inaccuracies and formatting issues.

### 2. Missing Certification Statement
A translation without proper certification may trigger an RFE.

### 3. Partial Translation
USCIS requires a complete translation, not selected sections only.

### 4. Missing Stamps and Seals
Official seals often contain important information and must be translated or noted.

### 5. Incorrect Names or Dates
Even minor inconsistencies can create complications during review.
